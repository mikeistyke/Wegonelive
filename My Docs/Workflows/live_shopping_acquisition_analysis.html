1 · Viral Coefficient (K-Factor) Epidemiological diffusion model; applied to products by Andrew Chen & Sequoia. K = (invitations sent per user) × (conversion rate of invites). K > 1 = exponential growth. Your current runbook relies entirely on you doing outreach. If K < 1, every event requires the same effort. Build K > 1 and events grow themselves. Offer "Bring a Buyer" incentive: existing attendees get $5 store credit for every new buyer they refer. Track referral codes per person. Target K = 1.2 minimum by event 6. K-factor per event
Referral conversion rate
2 · Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) Clayton Christensen, Harvard. Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to accomplish a job. The job has functional, social, and emotional dimensions. Your buyers aren't hiring you to "buy a vintage jacket." They're hiring you to feel the thrill of a live win, gain social proof ("I got the rare one"), and reduce the anxiety of not knowing what to collect next. Run 5 post-purchase voice interviews. Ask: "What was happening in your life that made you decide to join tonight?" The verbatim answer rewrites all your marketing copy. Map 3 JTBD jobs. Build one ad creative per job. Purchase motivation clusters
Message-market resonance score
3 · Diffusion of Innovation Everett Rogers (1962). Adoption follows a bell curve: Innovators (2.5%) → Early Adopters (13.5%) → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards. The "chasm" between early adopters and early majority is where most businesses die. Your first 50 buyers are Innovators/Early Adopters — highly motivated, tolerant of rough edges, intrinsically curious. Do NOT optimize acquisition for the mass market yet. Over-invest in making these 50 people feel like insiders. They become your bridge across the chasm. Create a named VIP tier ("Founding Buyers") for first 50 customers. Give them early lot access, name them on stream, and solicit their feedback publicly. This leverages social identity theory — people act consistently with the identity you assign them. Cohort 1 repeat rate
Referrals from cohort 1
4 · Behavioral Economics — Scarcity & Loss Aversion Kahneman & Tversky (1979): losses loom ~2× larger than equivalent gains. Cialdini (1984): scarcity is one of the 6 core influence principles. FOMO is a behavioral loop, not a tactic. Your runbook mentions urgency but doesn't distinguish real scarcity from fabricated scarcity. Buyers can sense artificial urgency and it destroys trust permanently. Only use scarcity you can prove live (show inventory count on camera). Use live inventory visualization: hold up the physical items and say "three of these in hand." Say "when this goes, it's gone — I won't be re-sourcing this." Frame unsold lots as "the one that got away" in post-event recap to build FOMO for next event. Lot sell-through rate
Re-engagement rate from FOMO content
5 · RFM Cohort Segmentation Recency, Frequency, Monetary model (Hughes, 1994). Proven in direct marketing, e-commerce, and subscription businesses. Predicts future purchase probability from 3 behavioral signals. Not all buyers are equal. A buyer who attended 3 events in the last month and spent $300 total is worth 10× a one-time $30 buyer in terms of acquisition cost amortization. You need a tiered model from event 1. After event 3, score every buyer on R/F/M (1–5 each). Top-tier buyers (score 12–15) get personal outreach before next event. Mid-tier (7–11) get the standard reminder sequence. Low-tier (≤6) get a re-engagement offer ("Haven't seen you — here's $10 back"). LTV by RFM tier
Reactivation rate from low-tier campaign
6 · Network Effects & Platform Economics Metcalfe's Law: value of a network scales with n². Applied to marketplaces by Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne (2006). Live shopping is a two-sided market: viewers and sellers. The more live viewers you have, the more valuable the experience is for every individual viewer (social validation, competitive bidding, chat energy). Viewer count is itself a product feature, not just a vanity metric. Low-viewer events are self-defeating. Seed early viewers strategically: invite 10–15 trusted contacts to join the first 3 minutes of every event, even if they won't buy, purely to raise apparent viewer count. This is not deception — it's social priming. Also: share live viewer count verbally ("We've got 60 people here tonight") to create crowd-pull effect. Peak concurrent viewers
Correlation: viewer count → conversion rate
7 · Content Flywheel & SEO Compounding Content marketing as a compounding asset (Dharmesh Shah, HubSpot). Each piece of content has a decay function; SEO/social content has a long tail. Replays are permanently searchable. Your runbook treats each event as standalone. Every event is actually a content factory producing 30+ repurposable assets: clips, product close-ups, buyer reactions, objection responses. These accumulate into an organic acquisition channel that costs nothing after production. After every event: extract 5 clips (30–60 sec). Post 1/day across the week. Use lot titles as searchable text: "Vintage Levi's Type II 1960s — Live Find." These rank on YouTube and TikTok searches from collectors who weren't at your event. Each clip is a passive acquisition touchpoint. Organic reach per clip
New-viewer attribution from content
8 · Community-Led Growth (CLG) Emergent model documented by Commsor (2021), validated by Discord, Figma, Notion. Community precedes product adoption; members acquire each other. CAC in CLG models approaches $0 at scale. Your highest-value buyers are not a list — they are a latent community waiting to be activated. A Discord or private Telegram group for returning buyers creates peer-to-peer acquisition: they bring each other to your events without your involvement. After event 3, invite top 20 buyers to a private "Collector's Channel." Post: lot previews 48h early, behind-the-scenes sourcing, community polls on what to source next. This serves 3 functions: retention, acquisition referral, and product research. Make members feel like co-curators, not just customers. Community-referred attendees
Community member retention rate at 30/60/90 days
9 · Attribution Modeling & CAC Optimization Multi-touch attribution (Berman, 2018). Not all acquisition channels contribute equally. First-touch, last-touch, and linear models each reveal different optimization levers. Without attribution, you optimize by intuition — and that's expensive. The runbook has zero measurement of where buyers came from. This means you can't double down on what works or kill what doesn't. By event 5 you should know: which channel produces the cheapest buyer, which produces the highest-LTV buyer, and which produces the fastest second purchase. Assign a unique join code per acquisition channel (DM outreach = "DM01", Instagram post = "IG01", referral = "REF01"). Ask buyers at checkout or in post-event survey: "How did you hear about tonight?" Map to channel. After 5 events, cut bottom 2 channels. Double spend on top 2. CAC by channel

Table 4 North Star Metrics — One Number Per Phase
Phase North Star Metric Why This One Target Benchmark
Pre-Event Acquisition Qualified Registrants (not just reach) Reach is vanity. A qualified registrant has taken an action (RSVP, DM reply, poll response) signaling intent. 100 qualified > 1,000 passive followers. Aim for 15–30 qualified registrants per event in H1; 50–80 in H2
Live Event Buyer Conversion Rate (Buyers ÷ Peak Viewers) Measures your live selling effectiveness independent of audience size. Industry benchmark for strong live shopping: 8–15%. Top performers: 20%+. Target ≥10% by event 3; ≥15% by event 8
Post-Event Retention Second-Purchase Rate (within 30 days) First purchase is an experiment. Second purchase is a behavior. A buyer who returns within 30 days has ~65% probability of becoming a long-term repeat buyer (RFM literature). Target ≥25% second-purchase rate by event 5
